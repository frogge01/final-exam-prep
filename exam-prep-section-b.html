Scenario 01 · Most likely exam question
CRLS1, OGD, Ceramide and Mitochondrial Membrane Potential
→ Stress-Induced Neuronal Apoptosis
🔴 Highest Priority — Friend 1's clue maps directly to this
💡
Section 1 — Hypothesis

We hypothesise that oxygen-glucose deprivation (OGD) causes downregulation of the enzyme CRLS1 in neuronal cells, leading to a reduction in cardiolipin levels. This loss of cardiolipin, combined with accumulation of the lipid ceramide, results in collapse of the mitochondrial membrane potential (ΔΨm) and triggers cytochrome c-dependent apoptosis (programmed cell death).

→ In plain English: When neurons are starved of oxygen and glucose (as happens in a stroke), the enzyme that makes cardiolipin gets turned down. With less cardiolipin, the inner mitochondrial membrane destabilises, the voltage across it collapses, and the cell starts killing itself.

📚
Section 2 — Rationale (Why we expect this to happen)

What is cardiolipin and why does it matter?
Cardiolipin is a special phospholipid (a type of fat molecule) found exclusively in the inner mitochondrial membrane. It plays four critical roles: (1) it holds the energy-producing protein complexes of the Electron Transport Chain (ETC) together; (2) it maintains the folded structures (called "cristae") that give the inner membrane its large surface area; (3) it physically grips cytochrome c — a small protein — keeping it inside the mitochondria, which prevents the cell from dying; and (4) it is required for importing proteins into mitochondria. CRLS1 (Cardiolipin Synthase 1) is the enzyme that builds cardiolipin inside the inner membrane.

What does OGD do?
OGD (Oxygen-Glucose Deprivation) mimics what happens to brain cells during a stroke. Removing both oxygen and glucose starves the cell of energy. This causes: a spike in toxic reactive oxygen species (ROS) — essentially chemical "rust" — which damage cellular components including lipids; a drop in ATP (energy); and disruption of normal lipid metabolism. The result is that CRLS1 expression decreases, meaning less cardiolipin is made.

What does ceramide do?
Ceramide is a sphingolipid (another type of membrane fat) that accumulates during cellular stress. It is pro-apoptotic — it promotes cell death. Ceramide disrupts membrane integrity, which accelerates the collapse of ΔΨm. Together with cardiolipin loss, it creates a two-hit attack on the mitochondrial membrane.

How does this lead to cell death?
When cardiolipin is lost or oxidised by excess ROS, cytochrome c detaches from the inner membrane, moves to the outer membrane, and is released into the cell's cytoplasm. In the cytoplasm, cytochrome c activates a "death machine" called the apoptosome, which activates caspases (enzymes that dismantle the cell from within). This is the intrinsic apoptosis pathway, and cardiolipin oxidation is the required trigger for it.

🔬
Section 3 — Methodology (Step-by-step experimental plan)

Cell model: SH-SY5Y cells — these are human neuroblastoma cells (derived from a nerve tumour) that are widely used as a laboratory model of neurons. They are relevant to Alzheimer's and stroke research.

  1. Induce OGD stress. Grow SH-SY5Y cells normally, then switch them to a glucose-free growth medium and place them in a sealed chamber with only 1% oxygen (instead of the normal 21%) for 6 hours. This mimics the conditions of a stroke. After 6 hours, restore normal conditions (called "reoxygenation") for 24 hours to model what happens when blood flow returns.
  2. Measure CRLS1 at the protein and gene level. Use Western Blot (WB) — a technique where you run proteins through a gel, transfer them to a membrane, and use a specific antibody to detect and measure the amount of CRLS1 protein. Also use qRT-PCR to measure the amount of CRLS1 messenger RNA (mRNA), which tells you whether the gene itself is being turned down or whether the protein is being degraded after it's made.
  3. Measure cardiolipin levels. Use a fluorescent dye called NAO (Nonyl Acridine Orange), which specifically binds to cardiolipin. Cells are stained with NAO and analysed by flow cytometry (a machine that measures fluorescence from thousands of cells quickly) or fluorescence microscopy. Less fluorescence = less cardiolipin.
  4. Measure mitochondrial membrane potential (ΔΨm). Use the JC-1 dye assay. JC-1 is a dye that fluoresces red in healthy, charged mitochondria (it forms "aggregates") and fluoresces green when the membrane potential has collapsed (it becomes a "monomer"). Calculate the red-to-green fluorescence ratio — a falling ratio means the mitochondria are losing their charge and failing.
  5. Measure ceramide accumulation. Use mass spectrometry-based lipidomics (a method that can identify and quantify all lipid species in a sample) to detect and measure ceramide levels. Confirm its location in the cell using immunofluorescence microscopy with an anti-ceramide antibody.
  6. Confirm cell death (apoptosis). First, separate the cytoplasmic fraction (liquid inside the cell but outside mitochondria) from the mitochondrial fraction and use Western Blot to detect cytochrome c — if it appears in the cytoplasm, it has been released, meaning apoptosis has been triggered. Second, measure caspase-3 and caspase-9 enzymatic activity (these are the "executioner" enzymes of apoptosis; elevated activity confirms apoptosis is happening). Third, use Annexin V / Propidium Iodide (PI) staining analysed by flow cytometry — Annexin V sticks to a molecule that flips to the cell surface early in apoptosis, and PI stains dead cells.
  7. Rescue experiment (the most important step — proves causality). Use a lentiviral vector (a modified virus used as a gene delivery tool) to re-introduce a working copy of the CRLS1 gene into OGD-treated cells that have had their CRLS1 knocked down. If restoring CRLS1 → cardiolipin levels recover → ΔΨm is maintained → cytochrome c is NOT released → apoptosis is reduced, then this proves that CRLS1 loss was the cause, not a coincidence.

Controls (essential — always include these explicitly in the exam):

Negative control
SH-SY5Y cells grown in normal oxygen and normal glucose medium — this is the healthy baseline to compare everything against
siRNA scramble control
When knocking down CRLS1 with siRNA, also treat cells with a "scramble" siRNA — a random sequence that doesn't target any gene. This ensures any effects you see are specifically due to CRLS1 loss, not the siRNA process itself
Positive control for JC-1
Treat cells with CCCP — a chemical that completely collapses ΔΨm. This confirms your JC-1 assay is working and can detect membrane potential loss
Vector-only rescue control
In the rescue experiment, transfect cells with the empty lentiviral vector (no CRLS1 gene inside it). This ensures any recovery you see is due to CRLS1, not the vector itself
📊
Section 4 — Analysis & Expected Results

If our hypothesis is correct, we would expect the following chain of results:

  • OGD cells show significantly lower CRLS1 protein and mRNA levels compared to control cells (confirmed by WB and qRT-PCR)
  • NAO fluorescence is significantly reduced in OGD cells, confirming cardiolipin depletion
  • JC-1 red-to-green ratio is significantly lower in OGD cells, confirming ΔΨm collapse
  • Mass spectrometry shows ceramide levels are elevated in OGD cells, correlating with the timing of ΔΨm collapse
  • Cytochrome c is detected in the cytoplasmic fraction (not just the mitochondrial fraction) by WB, confirming release. Caspase-3/9 activity is elevated. Annexin V staining shows increased apoptotic cell percentage
  • Rescue experiment: CRLS1 re-expression restores NAO fluorescence and JC-1 red ratio, reduces ceramide levels, prevents cytochrome c release, and significantly reduces caspase activity and Annexin V+ cell percentage — directly linking CRLS1 loss to the apoptotic cascade

Conclusion supported: OGD → ↓CRLS1 → ↓cardiolipin + ↑ceramide → ΔΨm collapse → cytochrome c release → caspase activation → neuronal apoptosis. CRLS1 is a potential therapeutic target for neuroprotection in ischaemic injury.

Scenario 02 · High priority
PINK1, Parkin and ΔΨm
→ Mitophagy Failure in Neurodegeneration (Parkinson's Disease)
🔴 High Priority — Clue C9 maps directly to this
💡
Section 1 — Hypothesis

We hypothesise that collapse of the mitochondrial membrane potential (ΔΨm) prevents PINK1 from being imported into mitochondria, causing it to accumulate on the outer membrane and recruit the enzyme Parkin, which then marks damaged mitochondria for disposal (mitophagy). When PINK1 or Parkin are lost or mutated — as in Parkinson's disease — this quality-control system fails, damaged mitochondria accumulate, and neurons progressively die.

→ In plain English: Healthy mitochondria destroy the PINK1 protein to show they're fine. Damaged mitochondria can't destroy it, so PINK1 builds up and sets off an alarm that causes the damaged mitochondria to be recycled. In Parkinson's, this alarm system is broken.

📚
Section 2 — Rationale

How PINK1 works normally:
PINK1 is a protein kinase (an enzyme that can switch other proteins on/off by adding a phosphate group). It is made in the cytoplasm and has a targeting signal that tells it to go to mitochondria. In a healthy mitochondrion with a strong ΔΨm, PINK1 is imported through channel proteins called TOM and TIM complexes. Once inside, it is cut up by two enzymes (MPP first, then PARL) and the resulting fragment is exported and destroyed by the cell's waste-disposal machine (the proteasome). So in healthy cells: PINK1 is constantly made and constantly destroyed. Its absence at the outer membrane signals "I'm healthy."

What happens when mitochondria are damaged:
When ΔΨm collapses (because of damage, toxins, or disease), PINK1 can no longer be pulled into the mitochondria — the import process requires the electrical charge to work. Instead, PINK1 gets stuck on the outer mitochondrial membrane. It activates itself (autophosphorylation) and starts tagging proteins on the outer membrane with phosphate groups, specifically a protein called ubiquitin. These "phospho-ubiquitin" tags act like flags that recruit Parkin from the cell's cytoplasm. Parkin is then activated by PINK1 and starts adding more ubiquitin tags (ubiquitination) to outer membrane proteins like MFN1, MFN2, and VDAC. This creates a "eat me" signal. Autophagy receptor proteins (p62, OPTN, NDP52) recognise these tags and connect the damaged mitochondrion to the autophagosome — essentially a bag that engulfs the damaged mitochondrion and delivers it to the lysosome for digestion. This entire process is called mitophagy.

Why this matters in Parkinson's disease:
Mutations in PINK1 (PARK6 gene) and Parkin (PARK2 gene) are the most common causes of inherited, early-onset Parkinson's disease. Without working PINK1 or Parkin, damaged mitochondria cannot be cleared. They accumulate, produce excessive ROS, fail to make enough ATP, and eventually trigger neuronal death — particularly in dopamine-producing neurons of the brainstem, whose death causes the movement symptoms of Parkinson's.

🔬
Section 3 — Methodology

Cell model: SH-SY5Y cells + Rotenone treatment. Rotenone is a pesticide that blocks Complex I of the Electron Transport Chain. This stops electron flow, collapses ΔΨm, and causes a surge of ROS. It's the standard lab model for Parkinson's disease, because it damages mitochondria in the same way that the disease does.

  1. Induce mitochondrial damage with rotenone. Treat SH-SY5Y cells with 5–10 µM rotenone for 24 hours. This collapses ΔΨm and mimics mitochondrial damage seen in Parkinson's disease. Confirm ΔΨm collapse immediately using the JC-1 dye assay (shift from red to green fluorescence).
  2. Detect PINK1 stabilisation on the outer membrane. In rotenone-treated cells, PINK1 should accumulate (the full-length 63 kDa form of the protein). Use Western Blot — the full-length PINK1 band should be much stronger in treated cells. Perform mitochondrial fractionation (separate mitochondria from the rest of the cell by centrifugation) and run Western Blot on each fraction — PINK1 should appear specifically in the mitochondrial fraction. Confirm location by immunofluorescence microscopy, looking for PINK1 co-localising (appearing in the same spot) with TOM20, a known outer membrane protein.
  3. Confirm Parkin recruitment and activation. Use co-immunoprecipitation (co-IP) — a technique where you pull down one protein (PINK1) with an antibody, and check whether Parkin is stuck to it. This confirms the two proteins physically interact. Use an antibody specific for phosphorylated Parkin (at the amino acid Serine 65) — this specific phosphorylation event is what activates Parkin. Visualise Parkin moving from a diffuse cytoplasmic distribution to concentrated spots on mitochondria using immunofluorescence.
  4. Confirm that mitophagy is happening. Use four readouts together: (a) Western Blot for LC3-II — LC3-II is a protein that gets incorporated into the autophagosome membrane. More LC3-II = more autophagosomes = more active mitophagy. (b) Check that p62 levels decrease over time — p62 is the protein that links ubiquitinated mitochondria to the autophagosome; when mitophagy is active, p62 itself gets destroyed too. (c) Measure total mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) by quantitative PCR — less mtDNA means mitochondria are being degraded. (d) Use MitoTracker (a fluorescent dye that stains mitochondria) and measure total fluorescence by flow cytometry — a decrease means mitochondria are being destroyed.
  5. Loss-of-function experiment (the key test). Use siRNA to knock down PINK1 or Parkin individually in rotenone-treated cells. If the hypothesis is correct, knocking down either protein should block mitophagy — confirmed by LC3-II NOT increasing, p62 NOT decreasing, and mtDNA remaining elevated. Additionally, these cells should show MORE damage: higher ROS (detected with MitoSOX, a fluorescent dye specific for mitochondrial ROS), higher caspase-3 activity, more Annexin V-positive (dying) cells.
  6. Rescue experiment. Re-introduce wild-type (normal, working) PINK1 or Parkin by plasmid transfection into cells where endogenous PINK1/Parkin has been knocked down. Prediction: restored PINK1/Parkin → mitophagy resumes → damaged mitochondria cleared → ROS decreases → cell death decreases.
Negative control
DMSO-treated SH-SY5Y cells (DMSO is the solvent for rotenone; gives baseline with no rotenone damage)
Positive control for ΔΨm assay
CCCP treatment — collapses ΔΨm completely, confirms JC-1 assay is working and sensitive
siRNA scramble control
Cells treated with a scramble siRNA sequence that targets nothing — ensures effects are PINK1/Parkin specific
Rescue vector control
Empty plasmid vector (no PINK1/Parkin gene inside) — confirms rescue effects are gene-specific
📊
Section 4 — Analysis & Expected Results
  • Rotenone treatment causes a significant drop in JC-1 red:green ratio, confirming ΔΨm collapse
  • Western Blot shows a strong full-length PINK1 (63 kDa) band in rotenone-treated cells vs. absent/faint in controls, confirming PINK1 stabilisation when ΔΨm is lost
  • Parkin phospho-Ser65 is detected only in rotenone-treated cells; immunofluorescence shows Parkin moving from cytoplasm to mitochondrial puncta (spots)
  • LC3-II levels increase and p62 decreases in rotenone-treated cells, confirming active mitophagy. MitoTracker intensity decreases, confirming mitochondrial mass loss through degradation
  • PINK1/Parkin siRNA knockdown blocks these responses: LC3-II does NOT increase, mitochondria are NOT cleared, ROS (MitoSOX) is higher, more cells die (Annexin V+)
  • Rescue with wild-type PINK1/Parkin restores mitophagy markers and reduces cell death, confirming causality

Conclusion: ΔΨm loss is both necessary and sufficient to stabilise PINK1 and initiate Parkin-dependent mitophagy. Loss of either protein (as in Parkinson's disease mutations) blocks mitochondrial quality control, causing accumulation of damaged mitochondria and neurodegeneration.

Scenario 03 · Possible integrative question
PACS2, MFN2, Rotenone and MAMs
→ ER-Mitochondria Communication Breakdown in Neurodegeneration
🟡 Possible — likely if George wants a cross-lecture integrative question
💡
Section 1 — Hypothesis

We hypothesise that rotenone-induced mitochondrial damage causes the protein MFN2 to be degraded by the Parkin pathway, disrupting the physical contact zones between the ER and mitochondria (called MAMs). Loss of MAMs impairs calcium transfer from ER to mitochondria and triggers the Unfolded Protein Response (UPR/ER stress), contributing to neuronal dysfunction in Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease.

→ In plain English: The ER and mitochondria in healthy cells are held close together by protein "bridges." Rotenone breaks one of the key bridge proteins (MFN2), the communication between ER and mitochondria fails, and this sets off an ER stress response that damages neurons.

📚
Section 2 — Rationale

What are MAMs?
MAMs (Mitochondria-Associated Membranes) are specialised zones where the outer mitochondrial membrane and the ER membrane come extremely close together — only 10 to 30 nanometres apart (roughly 3,000 times thinner than a human hair). They are held at this distance by protein tethers. The most important tethering proteins include MFN2 (on the mitochondrial outer membrane) and PACS2 (a multifunctional trafficking protein). MAMs perform critical functions: they transfer calcium from the ER into the mitochondria (which helps drive ATP production by the TCA cycle), they are sites of lipid synthesis, and they serve as platforms for mitophagy initiation. In Alzheimer's disease, MAM structure is disrupted early in the disease process.

What happens to MFN2 in disease?
MFN2 (Mitofusin 2) is a GTPase protein (an enzyme that uses GTP as fuel) embedded in the outer mitochondrial membrane. It does two jobs: it drives mitochondrial fusion (merging of mitochondria into networks), and it serves as a structural tether between the ER and mitochondria at MAMs. When Parkin is activated (which happens when ΔΨm collapses, as caused by rotenone), one of Parkin's main targets for ubiquitination and degradation is MFN2. So when rotenone damages mitochondria → PINK1/Parkin activates → Parkin degrades MFN2 → MAM contacts are lost. Clue C11 from your friend directly noted "ROT → ↓MFN → MFN deficient = aberrant morphology." Additionally, PACS2, which also stabilises MAMs, is disrupted in Alzheimer's disease.

What is ER stress / UPR?
The Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER) is where proteins are folded into their correct shapes. When something goes wrong — such as calcium imbalance (because MAMs are broken and ER can't offload calcium properly) — proteins start misfolding in the ER. The ER activates an emergency response called the Unfolded Protein Response (UPR). The UPR has three branches activated by sensor proteins: PERK, IRE1, and ATF6. One of the first markers of UPR activation is upregulation of GRP78 (also called BiP), an ER chaperone protein. Another key marker is spliced XBP1 (produced by IRE1 activity). If ER stress is prolonged and cannot be resolved, the pro-apoptotic protein CHOP is expressed, which triggers cell death.

🔬
Section 3 — Methodology

Cell model: SH-SY5Y cells + Rotenone (5 µM, 24–48 h).

  1. Induce mitochondrial stress. Treat SH-SY5Y cells with 5 µM rotenone for 24–48 hours. Confirm damage with ΔΨm (JC-1 assay), ATP quantification (using a luciferase-based luminescence assay, where ATP levels are directly proportional to light output), and ROS measurement (MitoSOX red dye).
  2. Assess MAM integrity. Use two approaches: (a) Co-immunoprecipitation (co-IP) of the IP3R–GRP75–VDAC complex — this is the main calcium channel complex that spans both the ER and mitochondrial membranes at MAMs; pulling down IP3R and detecting GRP75 and VDAC together confirms MAM contacts are intact; if this complex is disrupted in rotenone cells, MAMs are broken. (b) Electron microscopy (EM) — the gold-standard technique for visualising MAMs; you can literally see under a very powerful microscope whether the ER membrane is close to mitochondria or has separated.
  3. Measure MFN2 protein levels and degradation. Western Blot for MFN2 — expect a decrease in rotenone-treated cells. Confirm that MFN2 is being ubiquitinated (tagged for degradation) by co-IP: pull down MFN2 with an anti-MFN2 antibody and blot for ubiquitin — a positive result confirms Parkin is tagging MFN2 for destruction. Also check PACS2 localisation by immunofluorescence — in healthy cells PACS2 is enriched at ER-mitochondria contact sites; in rotenone cells it should be diffuse or absent from these zones.
  4. Measure ER-to-mitochondria calcium transfer. Use a mitochondria-targeted calcium sensor called mtCameleon (a genetically encoded fluorescent sensor that changes its fluorescence ratio when calcium enters the mitochondria). Stimulate the cells with a ligand that causes the ER to release calcium (e.g. ATP or bradykinin, which activate IP3 receptors on the ER). In healthy cells with intact MAMs, calcium released from the ER is rapidly taken up by mitochondria (detected as a large spike in mtCameleon signal). In rotenone-treated cells with disrupted MAMs, this transfer should be impaired (smaller or slower spike).
  5. Confirm ER stress activation. Western Blot for GRP78 (elevated = ER stress is active), phospho-PERK (the PERK branch of the UPR is switched on), and CHOP (elevated = the cell is heading toward apoptosis). Detect XBP1 splicing by RT-PCR — the spliced form of XBP1 mRNA (produced by the IRE1 branch of the UPR) is larger and runs at a different size on a gel, distinguishing it from the unspliced form.
  6. Rescue: overexpress MFN2 or PACS2. Transfect cells with a plasmid containing MFN2 or PACS2 coding sequence. If restoring these proteins rescues MAM contacts (confirmed by co-IP and EM), restores calcium transfer (mtCameleon), and reduces ER stress markers (GRP78, CHOP down) and cell death (Annexin V down), this proves MFN2/PACS2 loss was causal — not a side effect.
Negative control
DMSO-treated SH-SY5Y (no rotenone) — baseline healthy cells
ER stress positive control
Tunicamycin treatment — a drug that blocks N-linked glycosylation and causes strong ER stress; confirms GRP78/XBP1 antibodies and assays are working
siRNA scramble control
Scramble siRNA alongside any MFN2 or PACS2 siRNA knockdown experiments
Rescue vector control
Empty plasmid alongside MFN2/PACS2 overexpression, to rule out vector effects
📊
Section 4 — Analysis & Expected Results
  • Rotenone-treated cells show decreased MFN2 protein levels (WB) and increased MFN2 ubiquitination (co-IP), confirming Parkin-mediated degradation
  • PACS2 is absent from ER-mitochondria contact zones in rotenone-treated cells by immunofluorescence
  • co-IP of IP3R–GRP75–VDAC complex is disrupted in rotenone cells; electron microscopy shows ER and mitochondrial membranes are further apart than in control cells
  • mtCameleon calcium uptake signal is significantly reduced after ER calcium release stimulus, confirming MAM-dependent calcium transfer is impaired
  • GRP78 protein is elevated, spliced XBP1 is detected, and CHOP is upregulated — all markers of active and potentially harmful ER stress
  • MFN2 or PACS2 overexpression restores MAM contacts, normalises calcium transfer, and reduces GRP78, XBP1, CHOP, and cell death markers

Conclusion: Rotenone-induced ΔΨm collapse activates PINK1/Parkin, which degrades MFN2, dismantling MAMs and severing ER-mitochondria communication. This impairs calcium transfer and triggers ER stress, amplifying neuronal death. This mechanism is relevant to both Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease, where MAM dysfunction has been documented early in disease progression.
